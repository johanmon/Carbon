\section*{Introduction}

The concentration of carbon dioxide has increased during the
last century and we have very good data that shows us the
concentration on a monthly basis during the last sixty years. The
reason is, given the current narrative, that this is all due to our
burning of fossil fuel. This article will present some data that, at
least will make it plausible that, this is not the whole story. In fact
it might be that we are only responsible to a lesser degree - if this
is correct then it might have consequences. 

\section*{Mauna Loa}

We start by examining the measurements of carbon dioxide
concentrations in the atmosphere from the Mauna Loa observatory on the
island of Hawaii \cite{noaa:co2}. The data that we will use is the
mean monthly measurements from $1958$ to $2026$ (full years from
$1959$ to $2025$). If we plot these data we will see the classical
{\em Keeling curve} as shown Fig. \ref{fig:keeling}. If we look at the
changes during a year, in Fig. \ref{fig:year}, we see "the earth
breathing" - during the northern hemisphere summer, the growth in
vegetation decrease the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere
which is later released during winter.

One interesting fact to note is that the yearly natural changes has a
range of more than five ppm. We will look at the final increase over a
year that is around 2 ppm. The changes during a year is thus larger
than the increase that we will investigate further.

\section*{Emissions}

We of course need data on the worlds yearly fossil fuel consumption
and this is not something that can be measured. However, since
fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) are globally traded there are reliable
data on production and consumption, and for our use these will be
sufficient.

I've chosen the data obtained from "Global Carbon Budget" which is
provided by the University of Exeter \cite{gcb}. This compilation
consists of estimates of yearly fossil fuel consumption, divided by
source, from 1850 up to 2024 (the data for 2025 is not yet
provided). There are other compilations but they do not vary much and
for our purposes I think the data is accurate enough.

If we look at Fig. \ref{fig:gcb-emissions} we see the yearly emissions in
gigatons of coal. We can note that emission up until 1950 were quite
modest compared to our emissions today. If we look at
Fig. \ref{fig:gcb-accumulated} we see that the total accumulated emissions
between 1850 and 1958 is approximately 80 GtC which could be compared
to the emissions of 2024 alone that was in total 10 GtC i.e. today we
emit in one decade more than we have emitted between 1850 and 1958.

\section*{Correlation}

One question we might ask is how well our emissions correlate with the
increase on carbon dioxide concentrations. We can convert our
emissions, that are measured in gigatons of coal, into concentration in
the atmosphere, in ppm, by dividing by $2.13$ (standard procedure use
by the IPCC). Using the December-to-December increase as the response
variable we can then study the correlation.

In Fig. \ref{fig:correlation} we see a scatter plot with linear
regression of the emissions and atmospheric increase. We can note that
the emissions in general are twice as high as the increase seen in the
atmosphere. The correlation is however not very strong, an $R^2$ value
of $0.6$ is not very reassuring. An emission in one year of
approximately 3 ppm, corresponds to anything between $0.5$ to almost
$3$ ppm of increase in the atmosphere.  While the relationship is
evident, a substantial fraction of the year-to-year variability
remains unexplained. What is it that determines the yearly increase;
our emissions are - even though twice as high - not a good predictor
of the yearly changes in atmospheric concentrations.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \includegraphics[scale=0.6]{img/emissions-ppm.pdf}
  \caption{Correlation: emissions and atmospheric increase.}
  \label{fig:correlation}
\end{figure}

We could stop here and argue that there are a lot of things happening
but for sure - half of our emissions end up in the atmosphere. We could
also investigate things further; let's take a look at the temperature
over the same period.

\section*{Temperature}

We do not have any reliable numbers on the {\em global temperature}
but we do have several good estimates on how temperature has changed
over time. One source is "Global Land and Ocean Average Temperature
Departures" compiled and published by NOAA \cite{noaa:temp}. The
series extend back to 1850 but one must realize that the error bars
are quite large when we are talking about remote areas before the age
of satellites. We could also look at the series published by
University of Alabama in Huntsville that are based solely on satellite
data, but this series only extends back to 1979 \cite{uah:60}.

If we look at Fig. \ref{fig:temperature} we see that the two series
largely agree. They have chosen different time periods as their base but
to a large extent they do agree (UAH has a wider variance). Since the
carbon dioxide measurements extend back to 1958 we can start by
looking at the NOAA series.

\begin{figure}[]
  \center
  \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{../img/temperature.pdf}
  \caption{Global temperature anomalies}
  \label{fig:temperature}
\end{figure}

If we take the average of temperatures each year we can look at the
correlation with increase in carbon dioxide levels. The result is
shown in Fig. \ref{fig:temperature-ppm} and even though the correlation
is not very strong, $R^2$ is $0.72$ which is higher than what we had
for the correlation with fossil emissions. From a statistical point of
view, it would not be wrong to suspect that the temperature might
affect the carbon dioxide levels as much as our emissions.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \center
  \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{img/temperature-ppm.pdf}
  \caption{Correlation: temperature and atmospheric increase.}
  \label{fig:temperature-ppm}
\end{figure}

\section*{Regression}

Since we suspect that both emissions and temperature affect the carbon
dioxide levels we could of course do a regression analysis for:

$$ incr = k_t \times temp + k_e \times emi + const$$

The result is shown in Tab.\ref{tab:regression} and from this we would
conclude that the temperature is a far more important factor in
determining the increase in carbon dioxide levels. In this regression,
emissions are not statistically distinguishable from zero after
controlling for temperature; this is rather surprising.


\begin{table}[ht!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ c c c c c c  }
 \textbf{var} & \textbf{k} & \textbf{std err} & \textbf{t} & \textbf{P\textgreater[t]} & 95\% \\
\hline\\
const &         1.0013 &    0.238 &    4.198 &    0.000 &    [0.52, 1.48] \\
temp &          1.7765 &    0.407 &    4.369 &    0.000 &    [0.96, 2.59] \\
emi &          -0.0083 &    0.125 &   -0.066 &    0.947 &    [-0.26, 0.24] \\
\end{tabular}
\caption{Regression analysis.}
\label{tab:regression}
\end{table}

Before we draw any conclusions of this regression analysis we should
note that the temperature and yearly emissions are highly correlated
($0.95$). They both show an increase during the period and in a
regression analysis it might be hard to separate the two.


\section*{Statistical Model}

If we should trust the regression analysis the temperature is the
decisive factor if we want to predict the increase of carbon dioxide in
the atmosphere. Given the correlation shown in
Fig. \ref{fig:temperature-ppm} we can create a model:

$$ incr = 1.75 \times t + 0.99$$

The model can then be used to generate the yearly increases as shown
in Fig. \ref{fig:increase}. The generated series has a root mean square
error of $0.38$ and one clearly sees how it captures some of the
yearly changes. If we integrate the statistical model and start from
$315$ ppm the resulting reconstruction reproduces much of the observed
increase over the calibration period as seen in
Fig. \ref{fig:accumulated}.

As we can clearly see we capture the long term trend even though the

So we have two statistical models that both do a decent job of
recreating the observed carbon dioxide levels. The regression analysis
favors one but since the two explaining variables are so well
correlated it's hard to tell them apart.

Since we have data on both the temperature anomalies and emissions
from 1850, we could try to do a long term reconstruction. The carbon
dioxide level in 1850 is believed to be around $285$ ppm so we use
this as our starting point. As shown in Fig. \ref{fig:long} both models
overshoot the measured values even though the temperature model, shown
in Fig. \ref{fig:long_t}, does a far better job. The temperature model
will land on a calculated value of $470$ ppm, where the current level
is approximately $420$ ppm. The emissions model, shown in
Fig. \ref{fig:long_em}, will land on $712$ ppm which clearly shows that
the model does not work for longer periods.

The statistical models that we have now are of course not physical
models of what is happening. They happen to be good approximations of
the last sixty years but fails when we try to explain the long term
growth. 

If we try to create a physical model of what is happening we must of
course include emissions. For obvious reasons they do of course add
carbon dioxide to the atmosphere and this should mean something. We
could create a physical model where temperature does not play a role
but we would then say that the correlation that we see is just a
coincidence.

To see how unlikely this is we could do a Monte Carlo simulation of
carbon dioxide series. The increments that we see from year to year are
autocorrelated with a lag of one and we can model the series with the
following equation:

$$ {\rm inc}[i] = 0.65\times {\rm inc}[i-1] + 0.62 + \varepsilon$$

where $\varepsilon$ is a normally distributed variable with $\sigma =
0.56$. The residual of the measured series and the linear regression
is not normally distributed but close enough for our purposes. Our null
hypothesis is that the carbon dioxide level follows the given model
but is otherwise unrelated to the temperature.

If we collect the $R^2$ values of each generated series and the
measured temperature we can compare this to the observed $R^2$. We
then find, as seen is Fig. \ref{fig:monte}, that the observed $R²$ value is an outlier. The
generated series have a median $R^2$ value of $0.06$ and the
99-percentile is $0.33$. Given the observed $R^2$ value of $0.72$ we
can safely discard the null hypothesis.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \center
  \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{img/monte-r2.pdf}
  \caption{Monte Carlo simulation, dsitribution of $R^2$ compared to observed.}
  \label{fig:monte}
\end{figure}

The Monte Carlo analysis suggests that the observed relationship
between temperature and annual carbon dioxide growth is unlikely to
arise solely from the autocorrelation structure of the growth series.
The analysis suggests that the year-to-year increase of atmospheric
carbon dioxide is statistically connected to temperature.

A physical interpretation could be that the flow of carbon dioxide
between the oceans and the atmosphere is temperature dependent. The
question is if this process only means something on a yearly basis or
if it has long term consequences.

\section*{Discussion}

The analysis suggests that temperature may play a larger role in
short-term changes in atmospheric carbon dioxide than is often
assumed. Determining how much of the long-term increase can be
attributed to temperature-dependent processes remains an open
question. The correlation between emissions and temperature during the
period is too high to make it possible to separate the two
statistically.

In the end we want to construct a physical model of how carbon dioxide
in the atmosphere changes. This model should of course take emissions
into account but it should probably also have temperature as one
factor. It is quite obvious that the temperature affects at least the
short term changes and it would not be surprising if it also played a
role in the long term.

The analysis demonstrates a strong relationship between temperature
and annual carbon dioxide growth. Whether this relationship can
account for a substantial fraction of the century-scale increase in
atmospheric carbon dioxide remains unresolved.

If temperature is, if not the main driver but, partly responsible for
even the long term growth it will have consequences; any attempt to
limit the increase by controlling emissions might not be as effective
as generally assumed.
